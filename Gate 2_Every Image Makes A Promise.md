# Gate 2 — Every Image Makes a Promise
## 🧱 Foundational Truth
> ### People predict before they experience.

## 🎯 Objective

Every image makes a promise to its audience.

A promise is the audience's first understanding of the kind of experience they can expect.

The objective is not to promise the most exciting experience.

It is to promise the right experience.

When that promise accurately reflects what follows, audiences are more likely to feel rewarded, develop trust, and continue engaging.

## 🤔 Why It Matters 

> ### Once an image has earned attention, it begins shaping what the audience expects.

Audiences instinctively use visual signals to predict the kind of experience they can expect, whether they are choosing a film, a product, an article, or a social post. Whether intentional or not, every image shapes that prediction. Every image therefore makes a promise.

When an image makes an accurate promise, audiences gain confidence that the experience will align with what they expect. Repeatedly delivering on that promise builds trust, making creative a trusted signal for future decisions.

A **False Promise** occurs when the experience does not match the prediction the image created. While a False Promise may generate short-term engagement, repeated mismatches reduce confidence in creative as a reliable decision-making signal. Audiences become more likely to seek additional information before deciding to engage, reducing the effectiveness of the creative and weakening long-term trust.

By consistently making promises that the experience can keep, creative helps audiences make faster, more confident decisions. For businesses, this strengthens long-term engagement, improves retention, and builds stronger relationships with audiences.

## 👤 How the Audience Thinks

> ### People don't interpret images in isolation. They interpret them through what they already know.

Previous experiences, familiar patterns, and cultural conventions shape the expectations audiences bring to every image. These learned patterns help people quickly predict the kind of experience they can expect without consciously analysing every visual detail.

Once an image establishes those expectations, the audience naturally judges the experience against the promise the image made. When the experience aligns with that promise, confidence is reinforced. When it doesn't, audiences experience a **False Promise**.

This is why effective creative should reduce uncertainty about what the experience is, while preserving curiosity about what happens within the experience. **The goal is not to eliminate surprise. It is to ensure that every surprise feels earned.**

## 🧐 Gate Evaluation

A creative passes Gate 2 when the promise it makes accurately reflects the experience that follows.

👉 ***What promise is this image making, and does the experience deliver on that promise?***

A promise only succeeds when the experience fulfils the expectation it creates. If the promise and experience are misaligned, the audience experiences a **False Promise**, weakening confidence over time.

👉 ***What expectations is your intended audience likely to bring to this image?***

Audiences don't interpret images in isolation. They bring previous experiences, familiar patterns, and cultural knowledge that shape how they interpret what they see. Understanding those expectations helps reviewers evaluate the promise the image is actually making—whether it reinforces, reinterprets, or deliberately subverts those expectations.

👉 ***Would different members of your intended audience describe the same promise?***

If the intended audience interprets different promises from the same image, the creative is introducing ambiguity before the experience even begins. A strong promise should be understood consistently, even if individual reactions to that promise differ.

☝️ ***Remember:*** Gate 2 evaluates whether the promise is kept—not whether the experience itself is good.

## ✨Recommendation Framework

Every recommendation should explain **why** a creative decision is expected to improve the audience's experience, not simply **what** should change.

Structure recommendations using the following reasoning sequence:

### 📍 **Observation**

Describe what the image is currently communicating.

### 📍 **Reasoning**

Explain how audiences are likely to interpret the image and why this strengthens or weakens the promise.

### 📍 **Recommendation**

> **Recommend the objective, not the execution.**

Define what the creative needs to achieve before suggesting how to achieve it. Once the objective is clear, offer one or more possible creative approaches rather than prescribing a single solution. The strongest execution should always be determined by the creative team.

### 📍 **Desired Audience Outcome**  
Explain how the recommendation is expected to improve audience understanding, confidence, or decision-making.

### **Example** 

Instead of saying:

*Make the face bigger.*

Reason like this:

***Observation*** \
The artwork gives equal visual weight to multiple characters, making it unclear who the story centres on.

***Reasoning*** \
Audiences are likely to interpret the image as an ensemble story. However, the experience follows a single protagonist, creating a weaker promise about who they should emotionally invest in.

***Recommendation*** \
Strengthen the visual emphasis on the story's primary emotional focus so the promise more accurately reflects the experience audiences will receive.

Possible creative approaches could include:

* Increasing the prominence of the protagonist.  
* Simplifying competing visual elements.  
* Adjusting the composition to create a clearer focal point.  
* Using lighting, colour, or contrast to guide attention.  
* Reframing the crop to strengthen visual hierarchy.

The most appropriate approach depends on the creative concept and should be determined by the creative team.

***Desired Audience Outcome*** \
Audiences more quickly recognise who the story centres on, creating a clearer promise and greater confidence that the experience matches their expectations.


## 🧠 Reviewer's Creed

Creative should not promise the most exciting experience.

Creative should promise the right experience.

Trust is earned when the promise matches the experience.
